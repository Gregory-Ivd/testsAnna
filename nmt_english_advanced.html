Частина 4. Reading Comprehension — академічний текст

Прочитайте уривок і дайте відповіді на запитання. (Q24–Q28)

For the better part of two centuries, the figure of the lone genius has loomed disproportionately large in popular accounts of scientific discovery. From Newton beneath his apocryphal apple tree to Einstein scribbling in a patent office, the narrative casts breakthroughs as the work of singular minds operating, it would seem, in splendid isolation. The reality, as historians of science have patiently demonstrated, is markedly less photogenic.

Nearly every major advance, on closer inspection, turns out to rest upon a dense substrate of correspondence, collaboration and, not infrequently, outright dispute. Newton himself, for all his protestations of solitary labour, was an indefatigable correspondent; his quarrels with Leibniz over the invention of calculus, far from incidental, were instrumental in sharpening the very concepts at issue. Einstein's special theory of relativity, though bearing his name alone, drew on the prior work of Lorentz, Poincaré and others — a debt he himself was generally careful to acknowledge, even as posterity proved less scrupulous.

Why, then, does the myth persist? Part of the answer is psychological: stories require protagonists, and a heroic individual is easier to grasp than a diffuse network. Part is institutional: prizes, professorships and biographies all reward — indeed, demand — the identification of single authors. And part, more troublingly, is ideological. The myth flatters a particular vision of human achievement, one in which insight springs unbidden from the mind of the exceptional, owing nothing to the conditions that made it possible.
